Alpha Waves (8–13 Hz)

These are dominant when you're relaxed with your eyes closed. They decrease when you open your eyes or start thinking hard. Alpha waves are found mostly over the back of the head (occipital region), which handles vision!

From Brain Signal to Text — Step by Step

This is the journey your brain signal takes through our AI pipeline. Press the button to watch each step come alive.

1
Raw EEG Signal
A person looks at an image. 62 electrodes on the scalp pick up tiny electrical voltages (microvolts!) from neurons firing in the brain. We capture 440ms of data after the image appears — the window when visual processing happens.
62 channels 440ms window 1000 Hz sampling
2
Finding Patterns — Creating a Brain Fingerprint
Raw brain waves are too messy to use directly. We calculate how busy or active each brainwave type is for every electrode — like measuring how loud each instrument in an orchestra is playing. 62 electrodes × 5 brainwave types = 310 numbers that together form a unique fingerprint of this brain moment.
Δ delta θ theta α alpha β beta γ gamma
3
The AI Brain Translator
A trained AI looks at those 310 numbers and translates them — a bit like Google Translate, but instead of French → English, it converts brain pattern → meaning. The AI learned this translation by studying over 63,000 examples of people looking at pictures, until it could spot the hidden patterns connecting brain activity to what was seen.
Brain fingerprint (310 numbers) → Meaning code (1024 numbers)
Trained on 63,850 examples across 80 image categories
4
The Brain's Meaning Code
The AI outputs a list of 1,024 numbers — think of it as a GPS coordinate in a giant "idea map." On this map, similar things live close together — "dog" and "puppy" are neighbours, but "dog" and "airplane" are far apart. This coordinate tells us where in idea-space the brain was pointing.
AI meaning code — first 40 of 1,024 numbers shown
5
Turning the Code into Words
A text-writing AI takes that GPS coordinate in idea-space and asks: "What words live closest to this spot?" It then builds a sentence — a caption — describing what the person was most likely looking at. Like a detective piecing together clues, it figures out the scene from the brain's signals alone.
🖼️ Person viewed → an image of a sports car
About This Research

This is an ongoing Master's research project building a Brain-Computer Interface (BCI) — a system that lets a computer read brain activity. The goal: record brain signals while people look at pictures, then use AI to automatically write a sentence describing what they saw — using only the brain data, no cameras or eye trackers.

Why Is This Hard?
⚡

EEG is incredibly noisy

A blink, a muscle twitch, or even electrical interference from nearby devices can overpower the actual brain signal. Cleaning this noise is a major research challenge.

🧬

Every brain is different

The same image creates slightly different brain patterns in different people — just like two people's handwriting looks different even writing the same word. An AI trained on one person's brain often doesn't work well for someone else's.

📡

EEG has poor spatial resolution

Hospital brain scanners (fMRI) can spot activity in a region as small as a grain of rice. EEG headsets on the scalp pick up signals from millions of brain cells all blended together — much fuzzier, but they cost 100x less and you can wear them anywhere.

🗂️

Dataset limitations

In the data we used, pictures of the same category (like 20 dogs in a row) were shown back to back. This means the brain gets tired and bored partway through — and that tiredness leaks into the signal, making it harder for the AI to decode accurately.

Decoding Steps — Live
📡

Step 1 — Brain Signal Capture

62 tiny sensors on the scalp sense the electric buzz of brain cells firing as the eyes take in the image. We grab less than half a second of data — the exact time the brain is "seeing" and processing.

✓ Grabbed 0.44 seconds of brain activity from 62 sensors
🔬

Step 2 — Making a Brain Fingerprint

The messy brain waves get summarised: for each sensor, we measure how active each brainwave type is — like checking the volume of each instrument in a band. Result: 310 numbers that uniquely describe this brain moment.

✓ Brain fingerprint created — 310 numbers (62 sensors × 5 wave types)
🤖

Step 3 — AI Brain Translator

A trained AI takes those 310 numbers and translates them into a different type of code that language AIs understand — like converting sheet music into a Spotify playlist code. It learned this translation from 63,000 brain-recording examples.

✓ Brain fingerprint → meaning code (310 numbers in, 1,024 out)
🔍

Step 4 — Finding the Best Match

The meaning code is like a GPS location in idea-space. The AI checks: which known concepts (dogs, cars, mountains…) live closest to this coordinate? It ranks the best candidates — like finding the nearest restaurant on a map.

✓ Best matching ideas found from 80 known categories
💬

Step 5 — Writing the Caption

A text-writing AI takes the GPS location in idea-space and turns it into a real sentence — a description of what the brain was seeing. No camera. No eyes. Just brain signals → words.

🚗

Brain-reading technology is still young — but even today, researchers are finding real-world uses for it. Here are some of the most exciting places this could go.

Healthcare
🦹 Giving a Voice to People Who Can't Speak
People with conditions like ALS, locked-in syndrome, or paralysis lose the ability to move or speak — but their brain still works perfectly. A BCI headset could detect what they're trying to say just from their brain signals, and convert it into speech or text in real time. No movement needed — just thinking.
Early clinical trials underway
Gaming & Entertainment
🎮 Mind-Controlled AR/VR Games
Imagine playing a game — not with a controller, but with your thoughts. Just by focusing, relaxing, or imagining movement, your brain signals could control your character, cast spells, or navigate virtual worlds. The headset reads your mind; the game obeys it.
Active research & early prototypes
Neuroscience
💤 Recording & Replaying Dreams
During sleep, your brain replays experiences and creates vivid dreams. Japanese researchers have already decoded rough dream images from brain signals. Future BCI systems could capture your dreams as you sleep and reconstruct what you saw into images or video — like a camera for your sleeping mind.
Proof of concept published (2023)
Mental Health
🖤 Detecting Stress & Mental Health in Real Time
Your brain's electrical patterns change when you're stressed, anxious, or depressed — even before you realise it yourself. A wearable EEG device could silently monitor your mental state all day, alert you when you're overloaded, or help doctors track depression treatment over time — like a mood thermometer for your brain.
Wearable EEG products already on market
Creative Technology
🎨 Turning Thoughts Directly into Art
What if your imagination could paint a picture — without touching a brush? Researchers are experimenting with systems where the images you picture in your mind get decoded and fed to an AI art generator. Your mental image becomes a real painting. It’s early days, but the first demos are already working. Brain → meaning code → image generator → artwork.
First demos published — rapidly advancing with AI
🌎
The Bigger Picture

Every one of these applications starts with the same thing: understanding brain signals. That’s exactly what this research is building toward. By teaching a computer to read what the brain is seeing today, we lay the groundwork for reading what it’s feeling, imagining, and wanting to say tomorrow.
